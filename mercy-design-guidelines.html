About This Document

Approved project definitions are reviewed at each project kick-off meeting, resulting in selection of an appropriate prototype model. Minor modifications can be made within the prototype framework; major deviations require review and approval by Mercy Planning, Design and Construction and Chief Marketing Officer.

Primary Care Settings
Three Care Delivery Models

1Exam/Treatment Primary Care Clinics

▼

Services provided by physicians, nurse practitioners, and physician assistants specifically trained in first point contact with patients with undiagnosed signs, symptoms, or health concerns. Divided into groups of examination rooms and ancillary support services.

2Specialty Care Clinics

▼

Support Exam/Treatment Primary Care when specialized needs are prescribed. Includes Cardiovascular Services, OB/GYN, Oncology, Orthopedic, Surgery Medicine, Gastroenterology, Neurosciences and Urology. Special demands identified based on utilization and market demand.

3Primary Care Facility Types

▼

Freestanding clinics located within community settings. May include basic Laboratory and Radiology functions. Range from approximately 6,000 bgsf to over 12,000 bgsf. Covers both remote facilities and those located on Mercy Hospital Campuses.

Pod Concept
Flexible & Adaptable Design

Clinic pod prototypes are designed to flex for any specialty. Each pod provides a central core space with specific needs for specialty practices, limiting future renovation to this core area only.

→ Mix of large and standard exam rooms per pod
→ 6-foot corridor for patient traffic in each pod
→ Providers share pocket offices; MA staff in bullpen
→ Lab adjacent to MA work area for continuity
→ 29' standard column grid for 1-story clinics
→ 11' deep exam zones; 6' patient corridors
Governance Note

Approved project definitions are reviewed at each project kick-off meeting. Minor modifications are permitted within the prototype framework. Any major deviations must be reviewed and approved by Mercy Planning, Design and Construction and the Chief Marketing Officer.

The Mercy Experience

Five brand expression attributes that define how patients, families, and co-workers experience Mercy facilities.

Brand Values
Design Philosophy

The caring passion of the Sisters of Mercy is expressed through our actions and places. Each of the five brand attributes guides specific design decisions that create a uniquely Mercy experience across all clinic environments.

Vibrant

"Being vibrant is to believe that a single interaction with someone can change their mind or make them feel differently."
  • Abundance of natural light throughout the building
  • Create views that integrate exterior landscaping with interior spaces
  • Focus bright colors where attention is needed; aid wayfinding
  • Incorporate Mercy's brand elements unique to identity as a ministry
  • Include live plants whenever possible; draw the outside in

Easy

"Take away the worry or complexity for our patients and families."
  • Interior elements supporting intuitive wayfinding via material/finish changes
  • Open waiting spaces with visual connection between destinations
  • Locate family amenities close to the patient
  • Keep spaces clean and simple so people can find what they need
  • Always keep the patient moving forward through the clinic

Personal

"We are all individuals and we want to be recognized and valued for who we are."
  • Allow patients and visitors choice and control over environment
  • Offer a variety of seating: lounge, bar height tables, work counters
  • Plan for personal devices including charging capabilities
  • Create spaces supporting smaller groupings where a single family can wait

Comprehensive

"When patients feel all their needs have been met, we meet part of the challenge to get healthcare right."
  • View design through patients', visitors', and caregivers' perspective
  • Everything designed to meet the needs of patients and co-workers
  • Choose finish materials that stand the test of time in aesthetics, durability, and maintainability
  • Include all stakeholders in design process as early as possible
  • Balance needs of first cost with long-term maintenance

Professional

"Use our knowledge and experience to create trust and comfort and produce better outcomes."
  • Be good financial stewards of designed environments
  • Provide appropriate storage in appropriate places
  • All Mercy locations are clean, orderly, and well maintained

Facility Principles

Six core facility design principles that guide every Mercy clinic, from site selection to interior patient pathways.

Design Principles

1Easily Accessible Locations

▼

Mercy sites should be selected with easy, safe vehicular and pedestrian access. Sites should allow for good visibility from major intersections and roads and should include room for the facility to expand. This enables visitors to see the facility clearly, get to it safely, and navigate the site easily.

"Campus is easy to find – very accessible. It is located away from the congestion of traffic and business." – Springfield Orthopedic Focus Group

2Identifiable Exterior Features

▼

The unique elements of Mercy's exterior design are focused where those we serve spend the most time. The main element is the Mercy lantern — providing a welcoming beacon of light while drawing attention to the entry. The lantern is connected to Mercy's heritage; its design showcases a cross harkening to Mercy's Catholic Christian heritage and Catherine McAuley's habit of praying to window mullions.

"I like that the entrance is big and clear; I can easily navigate from any parking place to the main entrance." – St. Louis Focus Group

3Connecting the Outside In

▼

Mercy facility design connects inside and outside, bringing healing elements of nature into buildings through large expanses of glass in exterior walls and nature-focused artwork. Sites should have natural features enhancing experience. Freestanding clinics should always include the branded entry bench with scripture. Courtyards, outdoor waiting areas, seat walls, and water features should be incorporated where appropriate.

"Open bigger windows, not dark and gloomy." – Rolla Focus Group

4Comfortably Navigating Our Sites

▼

Mercy sites should be designed with clear wayfinding strategies, no-fault circulation, and clear decision points. On large campuses, this can be accomplished with a site loop road. Sites should be easy to navigate at a pedestrian level through hub and spoke parking and clearly marked pedestrian paths.

"The parking is well-arranged so that between front and back, one never has to walk very far." – Rolla Focus Group

5Security and Convenience of Entrances

▼

Mercy facilities should be designed with welcoming, comforting entrances offering patients a place to wait outside while sheltered under a canopy. Seating can be an integrated seat wall or bench. Entry vestibules should be designed with an L-shaped circulation path to block wind from entering.

6Intuitive Patient Pathways

▼

A goal in Mercy spaces is to prioritize patients' and staff's ease of navigation. This is accomplished by simplifying circulation through a main gallery combining circulation and seating. The large gallery allows views to destinations, making it easier to orient. Key circulation features such as elevators and stairways should be placed in highly visible areas.

"Signage should be easy to read and understand." – Springfield Orthopedic Focus Group

7Purposeful Interior Elements

▼

Mercy interiors create healing places that are warm, welcoming, and bright. Interior elements should be simple and utilize timeless design elements. Special materials draw attention to key elements. Bright colors should be used in locations that are easy to change over time (paint, artwork) while lasting materials (flooring, millwork) should utilize a range of neutral colors.

"A nice, clean facility tells me there is good care." – St. Louis Focus Group

8Authentic Use of Materials

▼

A key goal is to create places that are a natural extension of the community. This is accomplished by incorporating native landscaping and artwork of local natural features. Facilities should rely on natural lighting whenever possible to add warmth and connection to nature.

9Respect of the Mercy Heritage

▼

Interior spaces should be infused with the hospitality and warmth of the Sisters of Mercy. Design should anticipate the needs of the people who will use the space and preemptively address those needs — "they thought of me before I got here." The warmth of the Sisters is expressed by incorporating Mercy history and heritage items such as the Mercy cross and historical Sister photographs.

"You should use the Mercy symbols, the cross — more and larger." – St. Louis Focus Group

Facility Brand Identities

Mercy Brand Elements extend from the exterior into the interior and throughout the entire facility, creating a consistent expression of the Mercy identity.

Brand Elements
Brand Expression Philosophy

The experience patients have with us includes the entire 360 degrees of the encounter, impacted by our people, our places and the processes they interact with. The environment of care contributes greatly to the experience — these elements are designed expressions of the Mercy brand that uniquely articulate who we are.

🏮

The Lantern (BE007)

A beacon of light drawing patients and visitors to the entry. Includes a Cross embedded in the eyebrow or window mullions as reference to Catherine McAuley's heritage. Should be prominent and easily visible from the site access point.

🪑

Seat Wall / Entry Bench

Scripture is sand blasted into the face of the cast stone bench or seat wall. Scripture color should be natural with no paint or stain. Expresses comfort, healing, hope, and compassion in an inclusive manner.

🌿

Natural Environment

Establishing native plants and trees enhances and preserves the region while highlighting the beauty of God's creation. Research shows views of natural environments support healing. Primary focus of plant material is at entrances.

✝️

Mercy Crosses

Wooden crosses hung in staff spaces honor the legacy of the Sisters of Mercy. They remind co-workers to love and serve as Jesus did with the cross-within-a-cross design. Exact locations determined by the co-workers of each facility.

📜

Mission Statement (BE004)

Every Mercy place displays the foundational Mission Statement as if it is "built into" our very walls, just as it is "built into" the care we provide. Optimal location is the vestibule; if not available, a prominent location in the lobby.

🕊️

Crucifixes (BE005)

Displayed as a reminder that our Mission calls us to "bring to life the healing ministry of Jesus." Crucifixes are placed at the entries to clinic buildings on a prominent interior wall.

🪟

Scripture / Catherine Quotes on Glass (BE007)

Placed on clinic exterior windows and in public spaces, waiting and transition spaces. Catherine McAuley quotes used in co-worker spaces. Meant to be discovered as people circulate — providing comfort if needed or blending in if not.

🖼️

Artwork — Nature

Placed throughout the facility in waiting, transition, and exam spaces to create a consistently positive patient and family experience. Nature art promotes a sense of calm and healing.

🙏

Artwork — Religious

Shows the healing ministry of Jesus and reminds us of our call to serve. Placed in waiting and gathering spaces where people might pause and reflect.

Mechanical
HVAC Systems

Retail clinics: 1–2 RTUs with gas heat (where available) and 5–7 ton cooling capacity. Confirm sizing with mechanical engineer for all other prototype types.

Electrical
Power Service

Retail clinics: 120/208 volt, three-phase with 400-amp main circuit breaker. Confirm sizing with electrical engineer for all other prototype types.

Plumbing
Service Lines

Retail clinics: 1-¼" domestic water service; 4" PVC sanitary sewer service. Confirm sizing with plumbing engineer for all other prototype types.

Lighting Standard

All spaces: LED lighting not to exceed 0.75 watts/sf. Reference Mercy Lighting Schedule for specific fixture selections. Occupancy sensors required in exam rooms. Undercabinet lighting required at subwork and lab workstations.

LANDSCAPE Natural landscape features considered for enhancement of the patient experience
Technical Wayfinding
Building-Level Wayfinding

Wayfinding is to be thought of broadly as building elements, color, texture and pattern cues, as well as a coordinated concept established for signage and artwork.

  • ✦ Main gallery as primary spine and wayfinding anchor
  • ✦ Flooring material changes to define zones and reinforce direction
  • ✦ Color accent walls to identify zones and create landmarks
  • ✦ Bright colors at MA Work Area for emergency navigation
  • ✦ Elevators and stairs in highly visible locations
  • ✦ Restrooms and stairs findable without assistance
  • ✦ Clear signage throughout; reference Signage Guidelines
Technical Considerations Reference

For a more in-depth examination of wayfinding, signage, site, landscaping, and exterior architecture, designers should reference the Interior, Site & Landscape, and Exterior Architectural Guidelines for Clinics (separate document series).

Technical Standards

Space planning principles, structural standards, and leased space guidelines for Mercy clinic projects.

Technical Reference
Flexibility
Adaptable Design

Designers should maximize the use of flex spaces designed to accommodate a range of related functions. A standard planning module or column grid spacing throughout the clinic is encouraged. Standard column grid: 29' for 1-story clinics.

Efficiency
Operational Priority

Efficiency of long-term utilization, construction methods, and ongoing operations should be a key priority to each space planning exercise and design. Lean Design with central physician/MA work core.

Organization
Modular Clinic Concept

Prototype Concepts address modular construction including 11-foot deep exam zones and 6-foot patient corridor widths with minimal exceptions. Functional layering includes waiting, reception, exam rooms, and support areas.

Leased Space and Renovation
Community-Based Clinic Guidance

Because of their limited size and complexity, community-based clinics are often located in leased space and require renovation of existing spaces. The design flow and patient experience outlined in this design guide must be created in the design plans. Space program listings should be adapted for leased/renovated spaces using the defined workflow and room sizing standards.

Workflow Principle
Patient Flow Design
→ Patient enters through gallery / waiting
→ Checks in at reception; proceeds to intake
→ Roomed by MA through 6' patient corridor
→ Seen in exam room; provider works off-stage
→ Services come to patient in exam room
Staff Flow Design
Off-Stage Operations

MA Work Area — Central work core with counters and freestanding charting kiosks. In back of layout to provide buffer and prevent overhearing private conversations.

Staff Corridor — 5'-0" minimum width, separate from 6'-0" patient corridor, allowing staff to move independently of patient flow.

Shared Provider Offices — Located along back lane of suite. Two providers per office (typical 11'-6½"×6'-8"). Pocket office for Office Manager.
